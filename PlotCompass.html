Key principle

Not all data is created equal: some variables have more structure than others...

A variable's level of measurement determines what we can and can't do with the variable.

N

Nominal

What's the vibe?
Can be labeled, but cannot be ordered.
What math can you do?
Count: mode and frequencies.
Common examples
Eye color and ZIP codes.
<
O

Ordinal

What's the vibe?
Can be labeled and ordered, but distances between labels are unknown.
What math can you do?
Count + rank: mode, frequencies, median and percentiles.
Common examples
Education level and clothing sizes (S, M, L).
<
I

Interval

What's the vibe?
Can be labeled, ordered, and spaced evenly, but zero is only a reference point, not “nothing,” so ratios and statements like “twice as warm” are not meaningful.
What math can you do?
Count, rank, add and subtract: plus mean and standard deviation.
Common examples
Calendar year and temperature in Celsius or Fahrenheit.
<
R

Ratio

What's the vibe?
Can be labeled, ordered and spaced evenly, and zero means the quantity is completely absent.
What math can you do?
Count, rank, add, subtract, multiply and divide: including ratios and coefficients of variation.
Common examples
Height, age and goals in a soccer game.
Categorical variables: values are names of categories
Numeric variables: values count or measure something

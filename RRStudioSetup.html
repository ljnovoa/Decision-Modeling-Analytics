Step 2 of 6

Two tools, one workflow.

R and RStudio work together, but they do different jobs. You need to install R first.

2 min

R is the engine

R performs the calculations: means, probabilities, confidence intervals, tests, models, and plots.

  • Free and open source
  • Understands the R language
  • Does the statistical work
+

RStudio is the workspace

RStudio gives you a friendly place to write code, view results, make plots, and organize files.

  • Runs R behind the scenes
  • Keeps code and output organized
  • Makes learning R easier
Think of it this way: R is the kitchen that prepares the meal. RStudio is the organized counter where you keep the recipe, ingredients, and finished dish.

Step 5 of 6

Give your setup a test drive.

Run each example in the RStudio Console. Paste the result back here and the guide will check for the key values.

5 min

How to run code: Copy a code block, click beside the blue > in the RStudio Console, paste, and press Enter. These examples use R’s built-in mtcars data, so no extra download is needed.

1
Calculate a mean
Create a vector and summarize it
⌄
scores <- c(72, 84, 91, 88, 95)
mean(scores)

Expected: You should see a mean of 86.

Looking for 86.
2
Summarize real data
Explore a built-in dataset
⌄
head(mtcars)
mean(mtcars$mpg)
sd(mtcars$mpg)

Expected: A small table of cars, then mean MPG near 20.09 and standard deviation near 6.03.

Looking for values near 20.09 and 6.03.
3
Make your first distribution plot
A histogram of fuel economy
⌄
hist(mtcars$mpg,
     main = "Distribution of Fuel Economy",
     xlab = "Miles per gallon",
     col = "skyblue",
     border = "white")

Expected: The lower-right Plots pane should display a histogram with bars roughly spanning 10–35 MPG.

4
Run a hypothesis test
Compare automatic and manual cars
⌄
t.test(mpg ~ am, data = mtcars, var.equal = TRUE)

Expected: A two-sample Student’s t-test with t near -4.106, df = 30, and a p-value near 0.000285.

Looking for the t statistic, degrees of freedom, and p-value.
5
Fit a linear regression
Predict MPG from vehicle weight
⌄
model <- lm(mpg ~ wt, data = mtcars)
summary(model)

Expected: The coefficient for wt is near -5.344, and R-squared is near 0.7528.

Looking for the weight coefficient and R-squared.
